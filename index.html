About Me

I am a PhD student in Mechanical Engineering at Cornell University Sibley School of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering, studying point-of-care diagnostics and digital health, advised by Profs. David Erickson . I obtained my B.S. in Biomedical Engineering from The Johns Hopkins University with a focus area of medical device development.

I develop and clinically validate point-of-care molecular diagnostics that deliver laboratory-quality results in resource-limited environments. Specifically, I am interested in building integrated platforms, combining isothermal amplification, biosensors, and embedded electronics, and translating them into screening workflows for infectious diseases and viral-associated cancers in immunocompromised populations.

Please feel free to reach out to me via email or connect with me on LinkedIn!

    Robust Point-of-Care LAMP Diagnosis of Kaposi Sarcoma Temporally Validated in East Africa

    We validated a threshold-based, point-of-care LAMP assay for Kaposi sarcoma (KS) diagnosis using lesion biopsies from Ugandan clinics. A prior training study established a 26-minute amplification cutoff linked to KSHV DNA burden. In a temporally distinct cohort of 421 new patients recruited at the same sites, applying the unchanged cutoff preserved high diagnostic accuracy (sens. ~95%, spec. ~90%; ROC AUC â‰ˆ0.94), confirming that the threshold generalizes across patient populations and time.

    The platform (TINY) is rapid, energy-flexible, and suited to resource-limited settings; results support clinical translation and earlier KS detection where histopathology access is limited. Ongoing work includes simplified sample prep and internal controls to harden the workflow for field deployment.

    Ultrasound-EMG Fusion for Muscle Archetecture and Balance Control

    The ability to quantify how muscles coordinate to keep us upright is central to performance, rehabilitation, and fall-risk reduction. Today, balance assessment often depends on lab-bound force plates and clinician-dependent observations, while isolated EMG or dynamometry offer only partial views of the neuromuscular system. We built a portable ultrasound-EMG fusion system that continuously tracks muscle architecture (fascicle length and pennation changes) alongside electrical activation during both isometric and dynamic balance tasks. The platform combines real-time ultrasound processing with synchronized EMG to estimate joint torque trends and stability metrics without constraining range of motion or requiring force-plate instrumentation. In pilot human tests, the wearable setup captured contraction timing, fatigue-related shifts in activationâ€“architecture coupling, and balance-relevant features (e.g., sway-induced fascicle dynamics) suitable for athlete monitoring and neuro-rehab progress tracking. This approach enables richer, in-field datasets for clinicians, coaches, and device makers, supporting more precise interventions and faster iteration cycles.

    Skills: Ultrasound Imaging | Electromyography (EMG) | Sensor Fusion | Human-Subjects Testing | Biomechanics | Real-Time Signal Processing | Computer Vision (speckle/feature tracking) | Data Engineering (Python/MATLAB)

    Microendoscopy-based distal attachment system for the internal visualization of the Eustachian tube

    Accurate diagnosis of Eustachian tube dysfunction (ETD) is limited by the lack of tools that directly visualize the tubeâ€™s collapsed lumen. We developed a low-profile distal attachment that mounts on a 0.98 mm flexible CMOS microendoscope and threads through a standard guide catheter to gently cannulate the Eustachian tube, transiently open the lumen, and deliver in-situ images of the epithelium. In benchtop bovine-tissue models and a human cadaver, the system navigated tortuous nasopharyngeal anatomy, created a stable viewing pocket, and produced diagnostic-quality images. Image quality improved substantially versus conventional endoscopy, with a 112% increase in variance-of-Laplacian edge sharpness, while preserving >75% of the original field of view (checkerboard blockage test). By enabling live intraluminal visualization without pressurized insufflation or CT/MRI, this add-on platform supports more specific ETD assessment and could guide patient-specific therapy selection in routine ENT clinics.

    Multimodality Imaging of Angiogenesisâ€“Osteogenesis Coupling in Cranial Bone Repair

    Cranial bone defectsâ€”common after trauma or decompressive craniectomyâ€”affect >1 million Americans, yet standard PEEK reconstructions carry high complication rates and costs, and give little insight into how new bone integrates. To uncover the spatiotemporal biology of repair, we used a Pathak Lab multimodality platform to longitudinally image a murine calvarial defect in vivo/ex vivo, pairing intrinsic optical imaging of microvasculature with microCT reconstructions of bone. Across two integrated studies, I segmented and quantified vascular regrowth in 2D, then registered and reconstructed 3D defect volumes to track bone percentage volume over time.
